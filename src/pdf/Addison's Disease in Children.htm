Addison's Disease in Children

Addison's disease is when the adrenal glands don't make enough of two steroid hormones. The hormones are cortisol and aldosterone. Cortisol controls the body's metabolism, blocks inflammatory reactions, and affects the immune system. Aldosterone manages sodium and potassium levels. The adrenal glands sit on top of the kidneys. There is one gland on top of each kidney. Addison's disease is fairly rare and may first appear at any age.

What causes Addison's disease in a child?

The most common cause of Addison's disease is damage to the adrenal glands caused by an autoimmune disease. Other cases of Addison's disease are caused by damage to the glands by any of these:

  • Cancer
  • Infection
  • An autoimmune problem
  • A rare genetic disease

In rare cases, Addison's disease is passed down in a family.

Who's at risk?

A child is at risk for Addison's disease if they have any of the following:

  • An autoimmune disease
  • Cancer
  • Infection such as tuberculosis
  • Family history of the disease
  • History of using corticosteroid medicine
  • History of using antifungal medicine

Symptoms of Addison's disease in a child

Mild symptoms may only occur when a child is under physical stress. Symptoms may include:

  • Muscle weakness
  • Fatigue
  • Dizziness
  • Fast pulse
  • Dark skin, first seen on hands and face
  • Black freckles
  • Bluish-black color around the nipples, mouth, rectum, scrotum, or vagina
  • Weight loss
  • Dehydration
  • Loss of appetite
  • Intense salt craving
  • Muscle aches
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Intolerance to cold

These symptoms can be similar to other health conditions, so it's important to consult a healthcare provider for a proper diagnosis.

Addison's disease diagnosis

Diagnosis may include laboratory blood tests, an ACTH stimulation test to assess adrenal function, and measurement of electrolytes in the blood.

Treatment for Addison's disease

The goal of treatment is to replace the missing hormones and relieve the symptoms. Corticosteroid medicine, often taken for life, may be prescribed, and additional medication might be necessary to manage sodium and potassium levels.

Possible complications

If left untreated, Addison's disease can lead to severe complications such as:

  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Extreme weakness
  • Low blood pressure
  • Kidney failure
  • Shock from dehydration

Key points

  • Addison's disease is caused by insufficient production of cortisol and aldosterone by the adrenal glands.
  • The most common cause is autoimmune damage to the adrenal glands.
  • Treatment involves lifelong hormone replacement therapy.
  • Untreated Addison's disease can lead to severe complications, especially during physical stress.
